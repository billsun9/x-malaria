XMalaria: Automated Malaria Parasite Diagnosis

This web application provides an AI (or Machine Learning)-based method of detecting malaria-infected red blood cells from giemsa-stained thin blood smear images

Malaria Parasite Information

Malaria is a life-threatening mosquito-borne disease of global importance. After almost two decades of decline, malaria cases have significantly risen in 13 countries, according to the World Health Organization's 2018 World Malaria Report [1]. In 2017, there were 219 million malaria cases, compared with the 217 million in 2016. The related deaths in 2017 were estimated to be 435,000, prompting concern that more effective methods are needed to combat the epidemic. [1]

Malaria is caused by a protozoan parasite in the Plasmodium genus, the most lethal of which is P. falciparum. The most common biological vector for the parasite is the female Anopheles mosquito. As malaria parasites infect the red blood cells (RBCs), through blood transfusions, organ transplants, or contaminated needles, the disease can be spread from person to person. Thus, the prompt detection of malaria is essential for a patient to receive timely treatment and for preventive measures to be implemented to stop further spread of infection from mosquitoes or other means.

Two commonly used mechanisms of detecting malaria include rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs), in which test kits are able to detect the presence of malarial antigens, and analysis of “blood smears,” where a drop of a patient’s blood is spread out on a microscope slide, stained by some reagents (primarily wright-giemsa stain), and examined by a trained laboratorian [2]. This latter technique of microscopic diagnosis remains the gold standard for laboratory confirmation of malaria. Besides being extremely time-consuming and labor intensive, the results of such testing depend on the quality of the staining reagents, the resolution and magnification of the microscope, and the experience of the laboratorian, all of which can be lacking in tropical and sub-tropical regions where malaria is the most prevalent. Machine learning, a powerful statistical tool, may provide a paradigm-changing solution to this bottleneck.

Powerful Deep Learning Framework

Machine Learning is a very powerful statistical tool in which computer algorithms "learn" how to accomplish certain tasks without being explicitly programmed. In supervised learning, machine learning models iteratively improve their performance by training on a labeled training dataset. If trained successfully on a sufficiently large dataset, the machine learning models should be able to accurately predict on new instances outside of the original training dataset.

Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that utilizes deep neural networks (DNNs). Deep learning algorithms have found wide applicability in a myriad of fields, including in computer vision. This website employs two separate deep learning algorithms: an object detection model and a classification model. A entire giemsa-stained thin blood smear image is taken as input, and the YOLOv3 algorithm predicts out the bounding boxes for three possible types of objects in the image (red blood cell, white blood cell, and platelet). The classification model is a standard convolutional neural network and predicts if the red blood cells are infected or uninfected by the malaria parasite. The user is provided with zip files containing all segmented cells, predictions for bounding boxes on the entire slide image, and a data report.

Future Work

The classification model used in this study was trained on an NIH image dataset of 27,558 cell images with equal instances of cells infected by the P. falciparum parasite and those which were not [1]. Although P. falciparum is the most common type of malaria and is the most lethal, it is important to note that the classification model is unable to accurately predict for other less common strains of malaria, including classification of P. malariae, P. vivax, P. ovale, and P. knowlesi. In the future, new cell image datasets and model structures will be tested to further improve the predictive power.

Similarly, the object detection model (YOLOv3) was trained on the BCCD dataset [2], which contains 410 labeled Giemsa-stained blood smear images. This dataset is also rather small, and model performance could be improved by finding and incorporating new images.
